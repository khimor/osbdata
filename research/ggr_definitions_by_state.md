# GGR Definitions by State

How each state defines and calculates Gross Gaming Revenue (or equivalent) for sports betting.

---

## Arkansas (AR)

- **Term used**: Net casino gaming receipts = gross receipts from casino gaming less amounts paid out or reserved as winnings to casino patrons. Sports wagering combined with casino gaming.
- **Calculation**: Taxable Revenue = Gross Receipts - Amounts Paid Out/Reserved as Winnings
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly detailed in public reports.
- **Online tax rate**: 13% on net gaming revenue up to $150M per year per casino; 20% on revenue exceeding $150M threshold.
- **Retail tax rate**: 13% on net gaming revenue up to $150M per year per casino; 20% on revenue exceeding $150M threshold (same rate for retail and online).
- **Taxable base**: Net casino gaming receipts. Tiered: 13% up to $150M, 20% above $150M. Applied per casino license, not statewide aggregate.
- **Why it matters**: Two-tier graduated tax structure. Retail launched at Oaklawn Casino Racing Resort. Online legalized February 28, 2022. Relatively small market. Arkansas Racing Commission oversees. Four casinos authorized for sports betting.

## Arizona (AZ)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Event Wagering Receipts
- **Calculation**: Gross Event Wagering Receipts (Wagers) - Winnings Paid to Participants - Voided Bets - Federal Excise Tax - Promotional Credits/Free Bets (subject to sliding scale cap)
- **Promo deduction**: Sliding scale that decreases over time from launch (April 15, 2021): Years 1-2: up to 20% of gross wagering receipts; Year 3: up to 15%; Years 4-5: up to 10%; Year 6+: 0% (no deduction allowed). As of 2025, Arizona is in Year 4-5, so the cap is 10%.
- **Online tax rate**: 10% (privilege fee on mobile adjusted gross event wagering receipts)
- **Retail tax rate**: 8% (privilege fee on retail adjusted gross event wagering receipts)
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Event Wagering Receipts
- **Why it matters**: Separate rates for event wagering (10% online, 8% retail) and fantasy sports (different structure). Governor Hobbs proposed increasing to 45% for operators earning $75M+ per month. Sliding scale promo deductions phase out entirely by year 6 (2027). Both tribal and commercial operators participate.

## Colorado (CO)

- **Term used**: Net Sports Betting Proceeds (NSBP)
- **Calculation**: Gross Gaming Revenue (Handle - Payouts) - Promotional Credits/Free Bets (subject to sunset schedule) - Federal Excise Tax (0.25% of handle) = Net Sports Betting Proceeds
- **Promo deduction**: Promotional deductions undergoing major legislative change via HB25-1311: July 1 - Dec 31, 2025: max 2% of total free bets per month deductible; Jan 1 - Jun 30, 2026: max 1.75% deductible; July 1, 2026+: 0% - promotional deductions eliminated entirely. This represents a full sunset of promo deductions.
- **Online tax rate**: 10%
- **Retail tax rate**: 10%
- **Taxable base**: Net Sports Betting Proceeds
- **Why it matters**: Tax rate of 10% approved by voters in 2019 via ballot measure (Proposition DD). Promo deductions caused effective tax rate to be well below 10%. HB25-1311 phases out promo deductions completely by July 2026. Revenue directed to Water Plan Implementation Cash Fund. Projected $13M additional revenue from promo deduction elimination.

## Connecticut (CT)

- **Term used**: Online Sports Wagering Win/(Loss) minus Federal Excise Tax, minus Promotional Deductions
- **Calculation**: GGR = Wagers - Patron Winnings - Cancelled Wagers + Resettlements - Federal Excise Tax - Promotional Deduction
- **Promo deduction**: Yes - Promotional Coupons/Credits are deducted from GGR
- **Online tax rate**: 13.75%
- **Retail tax rate**: 13.75% (tribal compact terms)
- **Taxable base**: Gross Gaming Revenue (after promotional deductions and federal excise tax)
- **Why it matters**: Tribal-compact model. Only three operators: FanDuel (via Mohegan Sun/Mohegan Tribe), DraftKings (via Foxwoods/Mashantucket Pequot), and Fanatics Sportsbook. 13.75% rate applies to both tribal operators. Revenue split between tribes and state per tribal compacts. Limited operator competition compared to open-market states.

## District of Columbia (DC)

- **Term used**: Gross Gaming Revenue = Total cash or cash equivalents received from sports wagering minus the total of prizes and/or payouts.
- **Calculation**: GGR = Total Cash Received - Prizes/Payouts
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly detailed in public reports. Tax is calculated on GGR which appears to be before promotional deductions.
- **Online tax rate**: Class A operators (DC Lottery platform): 40% of GGR. Class B operators (private venues): 20% of GGR. Class C operators: 30% of GGR.
- **Retail tax rate**: Class B operators (private venue-based): 20% of GGR. Four stadium-based retail venues: Capital One Arena, Nationals Park, Audi Field, Northwest Stadium.
- **Taxable base**: Gross Gaming Revenue. Rates vary by operator class (A=40%, B=20%, C=30%).
- **Why it matters**: Complex multi-class license system. GambetDC (lottery-run app) was replaced by FanDuel in 2024. Class A (stadium/arena) rate increased from 10% to 20% on Aug 1, 2024. Council considering expanding to 8 district-wide mobile platforms under Class C at 30%. William Hill at Capital One Arena (Caesars) is primary retail operator. DC Council legalized December 2018.

## Delaware (DE)

- **Term used**: Net Proceeds = Total Sports Sales - Amount Won - Vendor Fees. This is the Delaware equivalent of GGR after vendor costs.
- **Calculation**: Net Proceeds = Total Sports Sales - Amount Won - Vendor Fees
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly detailed in public reports. Delaware Lottery manages the sports betting program; promotional costs likely handled within the vendor fee structure.
- **Online tax rate**: ~50% effective rate on sports betting revenue (state retains approximately 50% of net proceeds). One of the highest effective tax rates in the US.
- **Retail tax rate**: Same ~50% effective rate. Revenue distribution: ~50% State General Fund, ~10% racing purses, remainder as commission to racinos.
- **Taxable base**: Net proceeds from sports wagering. Delaware uses a revenue-share model where the state retains a very large share of all sports betting revenue.
- **Why it matters**: First state (besides Nevada) to offer single-game sports betting post-PASPA repeal, launching June 5, 2018. Lottery-managed model through Delaware Lottery. Online sports betting only launched January 2024 (nearly 6 years after retail). Only BetRivers available online. Three casino properties: Delaware Park, Dover Downs, Harrington Raceway.

## Iowa (IA)

- **Term used**: Net Receipts = Handle - Payouts (equivalent to Gross Gaming Revenue)
- **Calculation**: Net Receipts = Sports Wagering Handle - Sports Wagering Payouts. State Tax = Net Receipts * 6.75%
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly shown in reports. May be factored into net receipts.
- **Online tax rate**: 6.75%
- **Retail tax rate**: 6.75%
- **Taxable base**: Net Receipts (Handle minus Payouts)
- **Why it matters**: Currently tied with Nevada for lowest tax rate at 6.75%. Rate increasing to 9% effective July 1, 2026 (FY 2027). Iowa Racing and Gaming Commission oversees. In-person registration requirement was removed in 2021. One of the earlier states to launch.

## Illinois (IL)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Receipts (AGR) = Total Handle minus payouts to bettors. AGR and GGR are used interchangeably in Illinois.
- **Calculation**: AGR = Handle - Payouts
- **Promo deduction**: Illinois does not allow promotional credit deductions from AGR for tax purposes.
- **Online tax rate**: 20%-40% graduated scale
- **Retail tax rate**: 20%-40% graduated scale (same as online)
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Sports Wagering Receipts (graduated)
- **Why it matters**: Changed from flat 15% to graduated 20%-40% structure effective July 1, 2024. Additionally, a per-bet assessment took effect July 1, 2025: $0.25 per wager on first 20M wagers, $0.50 per wager beyond that. Generated 114% revenue increase vs old 15% flat rate. Chicago pursuing additional city-level levy.

## Indiana (IN)

- **Term used**: Sports Wagering Adjusted Gross Revenue (AGR)
- **Calculation**: Handle (total wagers) - Payouts on winning wagers - Adjustments. Note: Handle includes wagers received for future events where payout would be in a future month, so win percentage cannot be calculated by dividing revenue by handle for the same period.
- **Promo deduction**: Indiana law allows gaming licensees to deduct adjusted gross revenue attributable to free-play wagering. Total deductions per licensee are capped at $7 million annually.
- **Online tax rate**: 9.5%
- **Retail tax rate**: 9.5%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Receipts from sports wagering
- **Why it matters**: One of the lower tax rates, making Indiana competitive. Below neighboring states Ohio (20%) and Illinois (20-40%). Stable regulatory environment. Tax rate has remained at 9.5% since launch. Strong market due to proximity to Chicago metro area.

## Kansas (KS)

- **Term used**: Revenues = Settled Wagers - Payouts to winners
- **Calculation**: Revenues = Settled Wagers - Winning Payouts. State Share = Revenues * 10%
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly shown in reports. May be factored into revenue calculation.
- **Online tax rate**: 10%
- **Retail tax rate**: 10%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Revenue (Revenues column)
- **Why it matters**: Statutory 10% rate reduced to effective 6.5% due to generous promotional deductions. 80% of tax revenue directed to fund for attracting major league sports teams. Kansas Lottery oversees. Lawmakers argue eliminating promo deductions could boost effective rate without changing statutory rate.

## Kentucky (KY)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Revenue (AGR) = Settled Wagers Collected - Winnings Paid - Federal Excise Taxes
- **Calculation**: AGR = Wagers Settled - Winnings - Federal Excise Tax
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly shown in reports - appears to be factored into AGR calculation
- **Online tax rate**: 14.25%
- **Retail tax rate**: 9.75%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Revenue (AGR)
- **Why it matters**: Legalized via House Bill 551 (effective June 29, 2023). Separate rates for retail (9.75% at licensed tracks) and online (14.25%). Kentucky Horse Racing Commission oversees. Promotional wagers treated as regular wagers - not deductible. Racetracks and satellite facilities serve as retail locations.

## Louisiana (LA)

- **Term used**: Net Proceeds = Wagers Written - Payouts - Promotional Deductions
- **Calculation**: Net Proceeds = Wagers Written - Payouts - Promo Deductions. Taxes calculated on Net Proceeds.
- **Promo deduction**: Yes - Promotional deductions are subtracted and shown as a separate column
- **Online tax rate**: 21.5% (increased from 15% effective August 1, 2025)
- **Retail tax rate**: 10%
- **Taxable base**: Net Proceeds (Gross Revenue after promo deductions)
- **Why it matters**: Online rate increased from 15% to 21.5% under HB 639 (Act No. 298), signed by Gov. Landry. Retail remains at 10%. Revenue partly earmarked for SPORT Fund supporting NCAA Division I athletic departments at public universities. Parish-by-parish legalization model (55 of 64 parishes approved). A separate bill attempted to increase to 51% but did not pass.

## Massachusetts (MA)

- **Term used**: Gross Sports Wagering Revenue (GSWR) / Taxable Gaming Revenue
- **Calculation**: Total gross receipts from sports wagering minus (total winnings paid to participants + excise taxes paid pursuant to federal law). When GSWR is negative for a month, the operator may carry forward the negative amount for up to one year.
- **Promo deduction**: Promotional play deductions are allowed but details on cap or limits are embedded in individual operator reports. Negative revenue carryover is permitted.
- **Online tax rate**: 20% (Category 3 online/mobile licensees)
- **Retail tax rate**: 15% (Category 1 retail licensees)
- **Taxable base**: Taxable Gaming Revenue (Gross Sports Wagering Revenue)
- **Why it matters**: Massachusetts Gaming Commission oversees. Three license categories with different tax rates. Legislature considered bill to increase online tax rate but it remained at 20%. Strong market growth particularly around March Madness.

## Maryland (MD)

- **Term used**: Hold / Taxable Win
- **Calculation**: Handle - Prizes Paid - Promotional Play = Taxable Win. Hold = Handle - Prizes Paid. Taxable Win = Hold - Promotional Wagers - Other Deductions.
- **Promo deduction**: Promotional play is deductible from hold to calculate taxable win. No specific published cap on promotional deductions found in research. Public comment period on promotional play regulations was active in 2025.
- **Online tax rate**: 20% (increased from 15% effective June 1, 2025 via HB 352)
- **Retail tax rate**: 15%
- **Taxable base**: Taxable Win (Hold minus promotional play)
- **Why it matters**: Online rate increased from 15% to 20% under HB 352, signed by Gov. Moore. Original proposal was to double to 30%, negotiated to 20%. Retail remains at 15%. Revenue allocation: 5% to General Fund, 95% to public education. Promo deduction rules being tightened significantly.

## Maine (ME)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Receipts (AGR) = Gross Event Wagering Receipts - Voided/Cancelled Wagers - Winnings Paid to Players - Federal Excise Tax (0.25%)
- **Calculation**: AGR = GrossWagers +/- Adjustments - Voids - Payouts - FederalExciseTax(0.25%)
- **Promo deduction**: The AGR formula explicitly shows deductions for voided/cancelled wagers and federal excise tax. Promotional credits may be included implicitly via adjustments line, but there is no explicit 'promotional deduction' line item in the published reports.
- **Online tax rate**: 10%
- **Retail tax rate**: 10%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Receipts (AGR)
- **Why it matters**: Unique tribal partnership model. Caesars launched by partnering with three Wabanaki Nations: Houlton Band of Maliseet Indians, Mi'kmaq Nation, and Penobscot Nation. DraftKings also launched on the same date. One of the newer and smaller markets.

## Michigan (MI)

- **Term used**: Qualified Adjusted Gross Receipts = Gross Sports Betting Receipts minus the monetary value of free play provided to and wagered by patrons as an incentive or result of sports betting wagers.
- **Calculation**: Adjusted Gross Receipts = Gross Receipts - Free Play Deductions
- **Promo deduction**: Yes, fully deductible. Free play / promotional credits are deducted from gross receipts before tax calculation. This is a major policy point - Governor Whitmer has proposed ending unlimited promotional deductions (expected to add $21.1M to state budget).
- **Online tax rate**: 8.4%
- **Retail tax rate**: 8.4%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Sports Betting Receipts (after promotional deductions)
- **Why it matters**: One of the lowest online tax rates in the US. Governor Whitmer proposed ending unlimited promotional deductions in 2026 budget. Removing promo deductions estimated to add $21.1M to fiscal 2027 budget. Legalized December 2019.

## Missouri (MO)

- **Term used**: Taxable AGR (Adjusted Gross Revenue) = Total Handle - Total Deductions (which includes payouts, promotional play, promotional merchandise, and federal taxes)
- **Calculation**: Taxable AGR = Handle - Payouts - Promotional Play - Promotional Merchandise - Federal Taxes
- **Promo deduction**: Missouri allows sportsbooks to deduct up to 25% of their total taxable revenue for free bets and promotional credits given to incentivize gambling. This constitutional provision (from the 2024 ballot measure) has resulted in heavily negative AGR statewide during launch months. In Dec 2025 first month: $543M handle but negative AGR of -$21.6M for mobile due to massive promotional spending. A 2026 bill has been proposed to address this.
- **Online tax rate**: 10%
- **Retail tax rate**: 10%
- **Taxable base**: Taxable AGR (Adjusted Gross Revenue after all deductions including promos)
- **Why it matters**: Newest legal market (launched Dec 2025). Legalized via constitutional amendment in 2024. Generous promo deductions (25% of wagers) + loss carryforward have resulted in minimal tax revenue in early months. HB 3533 proposes adding 24% tax on adjusted gross receipts on top of existing 10%. Major concern about promo deduction impact on state revenue.

## Mississippi (MS)

- **Term used**: Taxable Revenue = Write (handle) minus payouts. Uses modified accrual basis calculation. Can be negative when payouts exceed wagers for a given sport/period.
- **Calculation**: Taxable Revenue = Write - Payouts (modified accrual basis)
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly detailed in sports wagering reports. Modified accrual basis calculation may incorporate some adjustments.
- **Online tax rate**: N/A currently - no statewide mobile sports betting. On-premises mobile only. Proposed: 22% on mobile sports wagering revenue (House Bill 4074, pending Senate approval as of March 2026).
- **Retail tax rate**: 12% total: 8% to state + 4% to local jurisdictions
- **Taxable base**: Taxable Revenue (modified accrual basis). Revenue derived from Mississippi Department of Revenue calculations.
- **Why it matters**: Retail/on-premise only. No statewide mobile/online betting. 26 brick-and-mortar sportsbooks operating. Bettors must be physically present at casino. Mobile betting allowed only on casino premises (geo-fenced). Online expansion efforts have stalled. Mississippi Gaming Commission oversees. One of earliest post-PASPA states but no online expansion.

## Montana (MT)

- **Term used**: Gross Gaming Revenue = Handle minus Payout (total wagers minus total winnings paid out)
- **Calculation**: GGR = Handle - Payout
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly stated in reports. Montana Lottery model - promo deductions likely handled internally by Intralot.
- **Online tax rate**: 8.5% of adjusted gross sports betting revenue
- **Retail tax rate**: 8.5% of adjusted gross sports betting revenue (same rate; retail kiosk-based model with mobile app requiring physical presence at licensed location)
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted gross sports betting revenue. Retailers (bars/casinos hosting kiosks) receive 6% commission on all bets placed.
- **Why it matters**: State-run monopoly through Montana Lottery. Must be physically at a licensed location to bet via mobile (on-premise mobile only, not statewide). Retailers receive 6% commission for hosting kiosks. First $12.4M annually goes to state general fund, excess to STEM scholarship fund. Major sportsbooks like DraftKings and FanDuel not available. Legalized May 2019.

## North Carolina (NC)

- **Term used**: Gross Wagering Revenue = Total Wagering Revenue - Cancelled/Void Wagers - Amounts Paid as Winnings
- **Calculation**: GWR = (Paid Wagering Revenue + Promo Wagering Revenue) - Cancelled/Void Wagers - Amounts Paid as Winnings
- **Promo deduction**: Promos are INCLUDED in handle (Total Wagering Revenue includes both paid and promo wagers). Promo wagering revenue is tracked separately but NOT deducted from GGR.
- **Online tax rate**: 18%
- **Retail tax rate**: N/A - online only
- **Taxable base**: Gross Wagering Revenue (before promotional deductions)
- **Why it matters**: One of the newest online markets (launched March 2024). Senate proposed doubling tax to 36% in 2025 budget, but House kept at 18% and rate remains at 18% pending budget agreement. Tribal sports betting existed before statewide online launch. Market exceeded revenue expectations in first year.

## New Hampshire (NH)

- **Term used**: GGR (Gross Gaming Revenue) = Handle minus payouts to winning bettors. This is the operator's net win.
- **Calculation**: GGR = Handle - Payouts
- **Promo deduction**: Not explicitly shown in published reports. The GGR figure appears to already account for any promotional adjustments. No separate promo deduction line item.
- **Online tax rate**: 51% revenue share (not traditional tax - state takes 51% of GGR)
- **Retail tax rate**: ~45% revenue share (varies by location)
- **Taxable base**: Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR) - revenue sharing model, not traditional tax
- **Why it matters**: Single-operator monopoly model with DraftKings. DraftKings pays 51% of mobile revenues in exchange for exclusive online license. If NH adds 1-2 additional online licenses, rate drops to 21%. Contract recently renewed through 2028. NH Lottery Commission oversees. First state in New England to legalize.

## New Jersey (NJ)

- **Term used**: Sports Wagering Gross Revenue = total amount wagered minus payouts to bettors. This is the 'win' for operators.
- **Calculation**: Sports Wagering Gross Revenue = Handle - Payouts
- **Promo deduction**: Limited. NJ Governor vetoed a bill that would have permitted broad sports-betting promo deductions. Operators can only deduct promotional credits exceeding $7.5M per month. The 8% Casino Gross Revenue Tax includes impact of eligible promotional gaming credits.
- **Online tax rate**: 19.75% (effective July 1, 2025; was 13% before)
- **Retail tax rate**: 8.5%
- **Taxable base**: Sports Wagering Gross Revenue
- **Why it matters**: Rate increased from 13%/8.5% to unified 19.75% on July 1, 2025. Original proposal was 25%, negotiated down to 19.75%. NJ was the state that brought the Supreme Court case (Murphy v. NCAA) that legalized sports betting nationally. One of the largest and most mature markets.

## Nevada (NV)

- **Term used**: Win = amount won by the house (casino/sportsbook), equivalent to GGR. For games, Win Percent is adjusted for effects of credit play. For slot devices, Win Percent is reported win amount divided by total dollar amount played by patrons. Win Amounts are in THOUSANDS.
- **Calculation**: Win = Total Wagers - Total Payouts (reported in thousands). Handle is NOT directly reported - only Win amounts.
- **Promo deduction**: Not separately broken out in public GRI reports. Taxable gaming revenue data is provided in an appendix (page A-01). Licensees file Monthly Gross Revenue Statistical Reports (Form NGC-31).
- **Online tax rate**: 6.75% of gross gaming revenue (one of the lowest rates in the US). Same rate for mobile and retail.
- **Retail tax rate**: 6.75% of gross gaming revenue (same rate for all nonrestricted licensees).
- **Taxable base**: Gross gaming revenue. Taxable revenue data in Appendix A-1 of monthly report. All gaming types taxed at same rate.
- **Why it matters**: Tied with Iowa for lowest tax rate at 6.75%. The original and longest-running legal sports betting market in the US. Nevada was exempt from PASPA (1992). In-person registration required to open online account. Nevada Gaming Control Board is the gold standard for gaming regulation. A 2022 ballot initiative to increase tax on revenue above $250K/month failed.

## New York (NY)

- **Term used**: Sports wagering gross gaming revenue is reported on a cash basis. Wagers on future events are taxed as current revenue and payouts for winning wagers are recognized in the period redeemed.
- **Calculation**: GGR = Total Handle - Payouts to Bettors
- **Promo deduction**: Promotional credits are reported but their deductibility from taxable revenue is limited. Net Revenue = GGR - Promotional Credits.
- **Online tax rate**: 51%
- **Retail tax rate**: N/A (different license structure for retail at commercial casinos, typically 10%)
- **Taxable base**: Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR)
- **Why it matters**: 51% is the highest online tax rate in the US (tied with NH, OR, RI). No promo deductions makes it uniquely punitive for operators. Overseen by NY State Gaming Commission. Multi-operator model (9 licensed operators). Despite high tax rate, market size makes it impossible for national brands to ignore.

## Ohio (OH)

- **Term used**: Sports Gaming Receipts per Rev.Code 5753.01(H): Gross Receipts minus Winnings Paid minus Voided Wagers minus eligible Promotional Gaming Credits. Currently, promotional credits are NOT deductible (deductibility begins January 1, 2027 at 10%, increasing to 20% on January 1, 2032).
- **Calculation**: Revenue = Total Gross Receipts - Winnings Paid - Voided Wagers (Promotional Credits NOT currently deducted but will be partially deductible starting 2027)
- **Promo deduction**: NOT deductible until January 1, 2027. After 2027: 10% of promotional credits deductible. After 2032: 20% of promotional credits deductible. Per Rev.Code 5753.01(H)(4)(a) and (b).
- **Online tax rate**: 20%
- **Retail tax rate**: 20%
- **Taxable base**: Taxable Revenue (Revenue floored at zero; losses not carried forward)
- **Why it matters**: Tax rate was doubled from original 10% to 20% effective July 1, 2023. Governor DeWine proposed doubling again in 2025 budget. Promo deductions banned until 2027, then phased in at 10% (2027) and 20% (2032). One of the fastest-growing markets.

## Oregon (OR)

- **Term used**: Gross Gaming Revenue = total wagers minus total payouts to bettors
- **Calculation**: GGR = Handle - Payouts
- **Promo deduction**: Not publicly disclosed. Oregon Lottery retains revenue share from DraftKings; promotional costs borne by DraftKings.
- **Online tax rate**: ~8% effective rate (Oregon Lottery revenue share model - not a traditional tax but a lottery profit transfer). Revenue directed to State Lottery Fund.
- **Retail tax rate**: Tribal casinos operate under compacts with separate revenue sharing agreements.
- **Taxable base**: Oregon Lottery profit model - lottery retains a share of sports wagering revenue. Funds directed to public education, economic development, veteran services, and responsible gambling programs.
- **Why it matters**: Oregon Lottery granted DraftKings exclusive monopoly for online sports betting in exchange for 51% revenue share. Replaced original SBTech platform in January 2022. Tribal casinos offer separate retail sports betting. One of the fastest-growing lottery sports betting revenue streams in the state.

## Pennsylvania (PA)

- **Term used**: Gross Revenue (Taxable) = Revenue minus Promotional Credits. Revenue = Handle minus payouts. Promotional credits ARE deductible.
- **Calculation**: Taxable Gross Revenue = (Handle - Payouts) - Promotional Credits
- **Promo deduction**: Yes, promotional credits are fully deductible from gross revenue to arrive at taxable gross revenue.
- **Online tax rate**: 34% state tax + 2% local share assessment = 36% effective
- **Retail tax rate**: 34% state tax + 2% local share assessment = 36% effective
- **Taxable base**: Gross Revenue (Taxable) = Revenue - Promotional Credits
- **Why it matters**: One of the highest flat tax rates in the US. Legalized in 2017. Despite 36% statutory rate, full promo deductions bring effective rate to ~23-25%. Funds support General Fund, property tax relief, and public programs. Lawmakers considering reducing or eliminating promo deductions.

## Rhode Island (RI)

- **Term used**: Book Revenue = Write (total wagers) minus Payout (total payouts). This is gross revenue before any commissions, operating, or allocable expenses.
- **Calculation**: Book Revenue = Write(Accrual) - Payout(Accrual)
- **Promo deduction**: No promotional deduction line item shown in published reports. Book Revenue is explicitly noted as 'before any commissions, operating, or allocable expenses.' Promo treatment unclear from published data.
- **Online tax rate**: 51% revenue share to state (not traditional tax)
- **Retail tax rate**: 51% revenue share to state (same for retail)
- **Taxable base**: Book Revenue (revenue sharing model)
- **Why it matters**: State-run model through Rhode Island Lottery in partnership with IGT and Caesars (formerly William Hill). Not a competitive open market. Second sportsbook expected to launch by end of 2026 per RFQ issued December 2025. Legalized June 22, 2018. Revenue split: State 51%, Casino 32%, IGT 17%.

## South Dakota (SD)

- **Term used**: Statistical Win = Handle minus payouts to bettors. Equivalent to GGR. Taxable Adjusted Gross Revenue further adjusts for non-cashable promo play, promo wins, wide area progressive deductions, city slots, expired vouchers.
- **Calculation**: Statistical Win = Handle - Payouts. Taxable AGR = Total Statistical Win - Non Cashable Promo Play + Non Cashable Promo Wins - Wide Area Progressive Deduction - City Slots (Net of Promo) + Expired Vouchers + Audit Adjustments
- **Promo deduction**: Yes. Non-Cashable Promo Play is deducted. Non-Cashable Promo Wins are added back. Wide Area Progressive deductions also applied. Detailed in Tax Calculation section of monthly report.
- **Online tax rate**: N/A - no statewide online sports betting. Retail only.
- **Retail tax rate**: 9% flat on taxable adjusted gross revenue (all gaming combined, not sports-specific). Distribution: 1% to State General Fund, 8% distributed per 42-7B-48 (40% Tourism, 10% Lawrence County, 50% Commission Fund).
- **Taxable base**: Taxable Adjusted Gross Revenue across ALL gaming types (slots + tables + sports). Sports wagering is NOT taxed separately.
- **Why it matters**: Retail only - no statewide online/mobile betting. Betting available at commercial casinos in Deadwood and at two tribal casinos (Sisseton-Wahpeton Oyate). Approved by voters in November 2020. Senate passed resolution to put online sports betting on 2026 ballot. South Dakota Commission on Gaming oversees.

## Tennessee (TN)

- **Term used**: TENNESSEE DOES NOT REPORT GGR. Tennessee taxes HANDLE, not GGR. The state switched from 20% GGR tax to 1.85% handle tax in July 2023.
- **Calculation**: N/A - Tennessee does not calculate or report GGR. Tax = Gross Handle * 1.85%
- **Promo deduction**: N/A under handle-based tax. Previously allowed under GGR model.
- **Online tax rate**: 1.85% (on handle, NOT on GGR)
- **Retail tax rate**: N/A - no retail sportsbooks
- **Taxable base**: Gross Handle (total wagers minus adjustments) - UNIQUE among US states
- **Why it matters**: UNIQUE: Tennessee is the ONLY state that taxes sports betting based on handle (total wagers) rather than operator revenue/GGR. The 1.85% handle tax replaced an earlier structure that had a 20% GGR tax with minimum hold requirements. Online-only state (no retail). Overseen by Tennessee Sports Wagering Advisory Council (replaced Education Lottery). High hold percentage nationally (9.23%).

## Virginia (VA)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Revenue (AGR)
- **Calculation**: Total Wagers - Cash Winnings - Eligible Noncash Winnings (first 12 months only) - Voided/Cancelled Wagers - Federal Excise Taxes - Certain Allowable Adjustments = AGR. Negative AGR may be carried forward to the following month for up to one year.
- **Promo deduction**: Eligible noncash winnings during an operator's first twelve months of operation can be deducted. After the first 12 months, promotional deductions are not allowed per the 2025 Appropriation Act (Chapter 725). This is a significant restriction compared to other states.
- **Online tax rate**: 15%
- **Retail tax rate**: 15%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Revenue (AGR)
- **Why it matters**: Online-only model (no standalone retail sportsbooks, though some retail locations exist at casinos). Promo deduction phase-out after 12 months was a pioneer policy adopted by other states. Virginia Lottery oversees sports betting.

## Vermont (VT)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Sports Wagering Revenue = total wagers minus total payouts, with adjustments. Operators remit their contracted Vermont Revenue Share based on this calculated amount.
- **Calculation**: Adjusted Gross Sports Wagering Revenue = Handle - Payouts - Adjustments
- **Promo deduction**: Promotional deductions likely factored into 'Adjusted' gross revenue calculation, but specific deduction rules not publicly detailed.
- **Online tax rate**: 31.7% operator revenue share on adjusted gross revenue (paid to state). Additionally, individual bettors pay 6% state income tax on winnings (7.25% for non-residents).
- **Retail tax rate**: N/A - Vermont is online-only, no retail sportsbooks.
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Sports Wagering Revenue. Operator fees: $550,000 per operator (paid once every 3 years). FY24 operator fees totaled $1,650,000.
- **Why it matters**: Online-only state. Legislation set minimum 20% revenue share, but competitive bidding drove rates higher: DraftKings/Fanatics agreed to 31%, FanDuel committed to 33%. One of the newest and smallest markets. Vermont Department of Liquor and Lottery oversees. Legislature considered per-bet tax and even prohibition of sports betting contracts.

## West Virginia (WV)

- **Term used**: Adjusted Gross Sports Wagering Receipts = Gross sports wagering receipts minus winnings paid to wagerers
- **Calculation**: AGR = Gross Receipts - Winnings Paid to Wagerers
- **Promo deduction**: No explicit promotional deduction mentioned in WV Code 29-22D. The definition of 'adjusted gross sports wagering receipts' only allows deduction of winnings paid. Promotional credits do not appear to be deductible from the tax base.
- **Online tax rate**: 10%
- **Retail tax rate**: 10%
- **Taxable base**: Adjusted Gross Sports Wagering Receipts (gross receipts minus player winnings)
- **Why it matters**: One of the earliest states to launch post-PASPA. No promo deductions, straightforward GGR-based tax. First $15M in tax revenue goes to state lottery fund, excess goes to state pensions. A bill (HB 4398) proposed increasing to 25% but has not passed. West Virginia Lottery Commission oversees.

## Wyoming (WY)

- **Term used**: GGR = Total Handle (wagers) minus Total Payouts (winnings paid to bettors)
- **Calculation**: GGR = Handle - Payouts
- **Promo deduction**: Wyoming appears to allow operators to deduct free wagers and promotional play from gross gaming revenue for tax purposes. Specific promotional deduction rules are in the Wyoming Gaming Commission's administrative rules.
- **Online tax rate**: 10%
- **Retail tax rate**: N/A (no retail sportsbooks)
- **Taxable base**: Gross Gaming Revenue (GGR), potentially after promotional deductions
- **Why it matters**: Online-only state (no retail sportsbooks). Small market. Lawmakers proposed doubling tax rate from 10% to 20% in 2025 but bill did not pass. Wyoming Gaming Commission oversees. Handle has quadrupled since launch.
